Media Agency Marketing Data Stack

The 7-layer infrastructure a media agency needs to plan, buy, measure, and prove ROI on media spend — from ingestion through reporting, with tool choices and tradeoffs at every layer.

17 tools across 7 layers
What this stack is

Most "marketing agency stack" guides cover the tools used to run campaigns — the ad platforms, the CRM, the email tool. This is a different guide. A media agency data stack is the infrastructure layer underneath those tools: the systems that ingest ad platform data, normalize it across channels, resolve identity across walled gardens, run measurement models, and push audience segments back to DSPs. Without this layer, media planning is guesswork. You can't tell a client which channel is actually working. You can't prove incrementality to the CFO. You can't reactivate their best customers with confidence.

The key distinction from a brand's internal stack: a media agency manages data across multiple clients, multiple ad platforms, multiple measurement methodologies, and potentially multiple clean room programs simultaneously. That means governance, multi-tenancy, and a clear data model are requirements from day one — not things you add later. Tools that work fine for a DTC brand's internal team often fail at media agency scale because they weren't designed for the multi-client, multi-platform, multi-methodology complexity that agency data teams live in daily.

The stack below covers 7 layers in order of dependency: ingestion first (nothing works without the data), then the warehouse (where governance lives), identity resolution (the hardest layer and the one most agencies under-invest in), transformation (Silver Step 1 — the most commonly skipped and most consequential step), measurement, activation, and reporting. At each layer we've covered the primary tool choices and the specific tradeoff that matters most — not a feature comparison, but an honest architectural decision.

The 7 layers at a glance
1 Ingestion Pull raw data from ad platforms, DCRs, and ACR data sources into the warehouse TapELT · Fivetran · Airbyte
2 Warehouse Store, govern, and serve all data — including clean room and Marketplace data Snowflake · BigQuery
3 Identity Stitch hashed email → RampID → UID2 → IP-based household matching LiveRamp · UID2
4 Transformation Normalize "impression" and "conversion" across channels — Silver Step 1 dbt · Snowflake Dynamic Tables
5 Measurement Attribution, MMM, incrementality testing, and clean room iROAS programs Measured · Rockerbox · Meridian · Snowflake DCR
6 Activation Push warehouse-modeled audience segments back to DSPs and ad platforms Hightouch · Census
7 Reporting Cross-channel performance reporting and client-facing dashboards TapClicks · Looker
The three decisions that define your architecture
Normalized ingestion vs. raw ingestion

TapELT normalizes ad platform data before it lands in the warehouse — conversion means conversion across Google, Meta, and TikTok. Fivetran and Airbyte give you raw platform data and you normalize in dbt. The former is faster to usable measurement. The latter gives you more control and more implementation work. The failure mode: teams choose raw ingestion, skip Silver Step 1, and then wonder why their MMM inputs don't reconcile and why the client asks "why does Google say ROAS 4.2 but our warehouse says 3.1?"

Deterministic vs. probabilistic identity

RampID (LiveRamp) gives you stable, persistent IDs across 60–80% of your addressable audience — best for batch measurement and clean room programs. UID2 gives you rotating deterministic tokens for logged-in users (40–65% coverage) — best for programmatic activation through The Trade Desk. IP-based matching fills the gaps probabilistically for CTV and linear environments with no login. The question is never which one — it's which combination for which use case, and always reporting match rate alongside every measurement result. A 55% match rate means 45% of the audience is invisible to the analysis.

Attribution vs. incrementality vs. MMM

These are not competing products — they answer different questions at different time horizons. Attribution (MTA) tells you about the path. Incrementality tells you about causality — what actually lifted. MMM tells you about portfolio efficiency across channels over time. For agencies managing $5M+ client spend, all three are necessary, and the work is reconciling when they disagree — which they will. The failure mode is treating these as substitutes: using MTA to make budget reallocation decisions that require incrementality data, or running MMM without two years of clean spend data.
