Data Clean Room Tools

6 platforms for privacy-safe data collaboration — infrastructure, purpose-built, and publisher-native. When clean rooms unlock deterministic attribution and when they're complexity you don't need.

Identity Layer
The category

A data clean room is a controlled environment where two parties can analyze combined datasets without either side ever seeing the other's raw data. In practice: a publisher has impression data, an advertiser has customer data, and a clean room lets them run overlap queries — who saw the ad, who bought — without either party exchanging raw records. The clean room enforces what queries can run and what the output looks like: aggregates only, never row-level. What changed in 2024–25 is that publisher-native clean rooms have matured enough to deliver deterministic attribution — not probabilistic estimates, but actual counts of which customers were exposed before converting. Netflix launched its Snowflake-based clean room in 2025; Amazon Marketing Cloud has been running since 2021. That shift moved streaming from a brand-awareness buy to a performance channel for advertisers who can prove the number.

Clean rooms come in three architectural flavors. Platform-native clean rooms (Netflix, Amazon Marketing Cloud, Google Ads Data Hub, Meta Advanced Analytics) live inside the publisher's ecosystem and let you analyze your data against their signals — deterministic attribution within that publisher's walls. Infrastructure-layer clean rooms (Snowflake Data Clean Rooms, AWS Clean Rooms) give you full control inside your existing data warehouse and let you define the collaboration rules yourself. Purpose-built clean room vendors (LiveRamp Clean Room, InfoSum) sit on top of those infrastructure layers and add identity resolution, partner connectivity, and pre-built query templates. Most enterprise teams end up with a combination: one platform-native clean room per major publisher for attribution, plus one infrastructure-layer clean room for cross-partner or first-party work.

Before you build a clean room, answer three questions. First: does your use case involve data from a party you can't share raw records with? If the data already lives in your warehouse, you don't need a clean room — you need a data model. Second: do you have the match rate? Clean rooms only work if both parties share overlapping identifiers — hashed email is the standard. If your CRM match rate against a given publisher is below 20%, the attribution output will be statistically thin. Fix the identity layer first. Third: what's your use case? Attribution and overlap analysis genuinely require a clean room. Suppression and lookalike targeting usually don't — a hashed email match table is sufficient for those. Full decision framework here.

The tensions in this category
Infrastructure control vs. speed to value

Snowflake and AWS clean rooms give you full flexibility but require data engineering to set up. Purpose-built vendors (InfoSum, Habu) get you running faster but at higher per-query cost and less control over the underlying infrastructure and query governance.

Privacy-safe vs. statistically useful

Differential privacy and aggregate-only outputs protect raw data but limit what you can learn. A query that requires 50K+ matching records to produce a result is safe — it's also often inconclusive for smaller advertisers or niche publishers. Privacy protections have a direct cost in analytical precision.

Clean rooms vs. identity resolution

A clean room doesn't solve your identity problem — it assumes it's already solved. If your CRM match rate is 30%, your clean room outputs represent 30% of the picture. Fix the identity layer first. A clean room built on a broken identity graph produces confident-looking answers to the wrong question.
